Title: Intellectual Property in Software Agreements

Software development agreements must carefully address intellectual property ownership and licensing to prevent disputes. The default rule in most jurisdictions is that the creator of a work owns the copyright, but this can be modified by contract. Work-for-hire provisions assign ownership of created works to the commissioning party, but their applicability varies by jurisdiction and the nature of the working relationship.

Pre-existing intellectual property, often called background IP, refers to technology, code, tools, and frameworks that a developer brings to a project. The agreement should clearly identify background IP and establish that the developer retains ownership while granting the client a license to use it as part of the delivered software. Without this distinction, disputes may arise about whether the client owns the developer's reusable tools and libraries.

Newly created intellectual property, or foreground IP, is the code and technology developed specifically for the project. The agreement should specify whether foreground IP is owned by the client through an assignment clause, jointly owned, or owned by the developer with a license to the client. Full assignment is most favorable for clients but may increase project costs, as developers cannot reuse the work. Joint ownership creates complications because each owner may independently exploit the IP, potentially competing with the other party.

Open source software presents additional IP considerations. If the developer incorporates open source components, the applicable licenses may impose obligations on the delivered software. Copyleft licenses like GPL require derivative works to be distributed under the same license terms, which can conflict with proprietary licensing intentions. The agreement should require the developer to disclose all open source components and their licenses, and should define acceptable license types.

Indemnification clauses protect each party against third-party IP infringement claims. The developer typically indemnifies the client against claims that the delivered software infringes third-party intellectual property rights. The client may indemnify the developer against claims arising from client-provided materials or specifications. These clauses should specify the scope of indemnification, notice requirements, and control of defense procedures.
