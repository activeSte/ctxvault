Title: Embedding Model Fine-Tuning for Domain Retrieval

General-purpose embedding models trained on broad web corpora often underperform when applied to specialized domains like biomedical literature, legal documents, or technical manuals. Fine-tuning the embedding model on domain-specific data can dramatically improve retrieval quality.

The most common fine-tuning approach uses contrastive learning with triplet or in-batch negative sampling. Training data consists of query-positive pairs, where the positive is a passage known to be relevant to the query. Hard negatives — passages that are semantically similar but not relevant — are particularly important for teaching the model to make fine distinctions within the domain.

Generating training data for embedding fine-tuning can be done synthetically. A large language model reads each passage in the corpus and generates several questions that the passage would answer. These synthetic query-passage pairs provide a scalable source of training signal without manual annotation. The quality of synthetic training data depends heavily on the diversity and specificity of generated questions.

Another approach is adapter-based fine-tuning, where a small adapter module is trained on top of a frozen base model. This preserves the general knowledge of the pre-trained model while learning domain-specific representations. Adapter methods require significantly less training data and compute than full fine-tuning, making them practical for smaller organizations.

Evaluation of fine-tuned embedding models should use held-out query sets with known relevant passages. Key metrics include recall at different cutoffs (R@5, R@10, R@100), mean reciprocal rank (MRR), and normalized discounted cumulative gain (nDCG). It is important to evaluate on queries that differ from the training distribution to measure generalization rather than memorization.

The deployment of fine-tuned models introduces additional complexity. The model must be versioned and tracked, existing document embeddings must be recomputed when the model changes, and A/B testing infrastructure is needed to validate that the fine-tuned model actually improves end-to-end system quality rather than just retrieval metrics in isolation.
