Title: Antibiotic Stewardship in Clinical Practice

Antibiotic stewardship programs aim to optimize antimicrobial use to improve patient outcomes while reducing the emergence of resistant organisms. Antimicrobial resistance is recognized as one of the greatest threats to global public health, driven largely by the overuse and misuse of antibiotics in both human medicine and agriculture.

Core strategies of antibiotic stewardship include prospective audit with intervention and feedback, where infectious disease specialists review ongoing antibiotic prescriptions and provide recommendations for optimization. This approach has been shown to reduce antibiotic use by 20 to 40 percent without adversely affecting patient outcomes. Formulary restriction and preauthorization require prescribers to obtain approval before using certain broad-spectrum or high-cost antibiotics, ensuring these agents are reserved for situations where they are truly needed.

Empiric therapy guidelines help clinicians select appropriate initial antibiotic regimens based on the suspected infection site, likely pathogens, and local resistance patterns. These guidelines should be developed collaboratively by infectious disease physicians, pharmacists, and microbiologists, and should be updated regularly based on institutional antibiogram data. De-escalation protocols guide the transition from broad-spectrum empiric therapy to targeted narrow-spectrum therapy once culture and sensitivity results are available.

Duration optimization is another key stewardship intervention. Clinical trials have demonstrated that shorter antibiotic courses are as effective as longer courses for many common infections. Community-acquired pneumonia can be treated for 5 days rather than the traditional 7 to 10 days. Urinary tract infections in women typically require only 3 to 5 days of therapy. Reducing treatment duration decreases antibiotic exposure, side effects, and the selective pressure for resistance development.

Diagnostic stewardship complements antibiotic stewardship by improving the accuracy and timeliness of infection diagnosis. Procalcitonin levels can help distinguish bacterial from viral respiratory infections and guide antibiotic discontinuation. Rapid molecular diagnostics identify pathogens and resistance genes within hours rather than the days required for traditional cultures, enabling earlier targeted therapy.

Education and training for all prescribers are essential for sustained stewardship success. Programs should cover principles of antimicrobial pharmacology, resistance mechanisms, diagnostic interpretation, and guideline-based prescribing. Feedback reports showing individual prescribing patterns compared to peers and guidelines have been shown to modify behavior and reduce inappropriate antibiotic use.
